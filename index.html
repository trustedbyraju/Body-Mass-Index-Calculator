What is Body Mass Index (BMI)?

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a simple numerical measure that uses a person's height and weight to estimate body fat and screen for potential weight-related health risks. First developed by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in the 19th century, it remains one of the most widely used tools in public health and clinical practice.

While BMI does not directly measure body fat percentage, it provides a quick, cost-free screening tool that healthcare professionals use alongside other assessments to evaluate an individual's overall health status.

BMI Formula Explanation

BMI = weight (kg) ÷ height² (m²)

BMI = (weight (lbs) × 703) ÷ height² (in²)

Example (metric): A person weighing 70 kg and 1.75 m tall: BMI = 70 ÷ (1.75 × 1.75) = 22.9 → Normal weight.

Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about Body Mass Index.

A normal BMI range is between 18.5 and 24.9. This range is associated with the lowest risk of weight-related health complications for most adults. Values below 18.5 indicate underweight, 25–29.9 is overweight, and 30 or above is classified as obese according to WHO guidelines.
BMI uses the formula: BMI = weight (kg) ÷ height² (m²). For imperial measurements: BMI = (weight in lbs × 703) ÷ height² (in²). For example, a person weighing 70 kg and standing 1.75 m tall would have a BMI of 70 ÷ (1.75 × 1.75) = 22.9.
BMI is a useful general screening tool, but it has limitations. It may overestimate body fat in athletes and very muscular individuals, and underestimate it in older adults or those with low muscle mass. It also doesn't account for fat distribution. For a comprehensive health assessment, always consult a qualified healthcare professional.
A BMI of 30 or above is classified as obese. There are three severity classes: Class I (30–34.9) — increased health risk; Class II (35–39.9) — high risk; Class III / Morbid Obesity (40+) — very high risk. Obesity is associated with higher rates of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, sleep apnea, and certain cancers.
This calculator is designed for adults aged 18 and older. BMI interpretation for children and teenagers is different — it uses age- and sex-specific percentiles rather than fixed cut-off points. For paediatric BMI assessment, please consult a paediatrician or use a specialised child BMI tool.
For most adults, checking BMI every 1–3 months is sufficient for general health monitoring. If you are actively managing your weight through a diet or exercise programme, monthly checks can help track progress. Remember to use BMI alongside other indicators such as waist circumference, blood pressure, and regular physical activity for a complete picture of your health.
